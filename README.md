# ai201-project4-provenance-guard

## Architecture overview

the path a submission takes from input to transparency label
The piece of text will be accepted by an API endpoint or Content Submission Endpoint. The submission endpoint has a rate limiting, therefore before the user submits a piece of text, the endpoint first passes through the rate limiter, checks whether the number of submissions allowed has been exceeded, or if the size of the text is within the limit, if so, a rate limit will return, and the detction will not run. When the text goes into the API, it goes through the multi-signal detection pipeline - the LLM based classification Groq and Stylometric heuristics. After the text is evaluated, a confidence score gets generated classifying if the model thinks the piece of text is generated by humans or AI.

After the confidence scores get generated, a combined score is calculated. Then the transparency label is created and displayed to an user on the platform, which communicates the attribution result with three label variants (high-confidence AI, high-confidence human, uncertain), as well as current status (Ex. completed).

## Detection signals

what each signal measures, why you chose it, and what it misses
**LLM-based classification (Groq)**:

What property of the text it measures: Assess whether the text reads as human or AI generated based on the overall semantic, structural, and stylistic patterns

Why that property differs between human and AI writing: AI generated text often exhibits recurring stylistic patterns, highly consistent organizations, and predictable phrasing learned from large training datasets. While human writing may contain individual variation, imperfect flow, and context specific stylistic word choices

What it can't capture (blind spot): The classifier may misclassify highly polished human writing as AI generated, or fail to detect AI generated text that has been heavily edited by humans as it relies on overall writing patterns for evaluation

**Stylometric heuristic**:
What property of the text it measures: Measures statistical properties of the writing - which includes sentence length variable, typo-token ratio (vocabulary diversity), punctuation density, or average sentence complexity

Why that property differs between human and AI writing: AI generated text tends to exhibit a more structured, formal style, lacking the idiosyncrasies of human writing process, and human writing is typically more variable. AI tends to produce text with fewer typos and errors compare to humans. Sometimes AI may uses uncommon vocabulary and formal phrases that are not typical in human conversations.

What it can't capture (blindspot): The heuristics detection mode captures only the statistical writing patterns rather than semantic understanding or context of the text, making them sensitive to differences in writing style, genre, and editing. Therefore stylometric features alone can be inefficient and must be combined with other detection signals.

## Confidence scoring

why this scoring approach, what you'd change if you were deploying this for real.
How I combined signals into a score, how you validated it's meaningful, and two example submissions with noticeably different confidence scores (one high-confidence, one lower-confidence) showing the actual scores

The final score will be calculated using a weighted average, using the following:

```text
combined_score = (0.6 * llm_ai_score) + (0.4 * stylometric_ai_score)
```

**How I combined the signals.** Each signal independently returns an AI-likelihood between 0.00 and 1.00. The LLM carries the larger weight (0.6) because it evaluates overall semantics and structure and was the more reliable discriminator in testing; the stylometric heuristic (0.4) is a supporting check that catches cases the LLM misses and surfaces disagreement. The weighted average maps to a label using the uncertainty-representation thresholds:

| Combined score | Label                |
| -------------- | -------------------- |
| 0.75 – 1.00    | Likely AI-generated  |
| 0.40 – 0.74    | Uncertain            |
| 0.00 – 0.39    | Likely Human-written |

**How I validated it's meaningful.** I ran the signals over clearly different inputs (`test_confidence_scoring.py`). The combined score showed a spread of more than 0.5 between a polished, uniform paragraph and a casual, irregular one, and all three label categories are reachable — confirmed deterministically.

**Two example submissions (actual scores from the audit log):**

- **High-confidence (AI):** a uniform corporate paragraph → `llm_score 0.85`, `sh_score 0.66` → **combined 0.77** → *Likely AI-generated*.
- **Lower-confidence (Human):** a casual review with slang → `llm_score 0.20`, `sh_score 0.13` → **combined 0.17** → *Likely Human-written*.

**What I'd change for real deployment.** The stylometric signal lags modern, fluent AI text, so I'd recalibrate it (length-normalized vocabulary diversity, stronger burstiness features) or down-weight it further; add score calibration so the numbers reflect true probabilities; persist data in a production database with a mutable per-content status field instead of an append-only log; and build human-review tooling for the appeal queue.


## Transparency label

Include a typed description of all three variants (high-confidence AI, high-confidence human, uncertain) — write out the exact text each one displays. You're welcome to include a screenshot or mockup as well, but the written description is what's required.

The transparency label is chosen by the combined score's band. The three variants and the exact text each one displays:

**Likely AI-Generated** (combined 0.75 – 1.00):

> The system found **strong evidence** that the content was generated or substantially assisted by AI and has **high confidence**. If you believe the result is incorrect, you may submit an appeal for review.

**Uncertain** (combined 0.40 – 0.74):

> The system found **mixed evidence**, and we couldn't confidently determine whether the content is AI-generated or human-written. If you are the creator and believe this result requires review, you may submit an appeal.

**Likely Human-Generated** (combined 0.00 – 0.39):

> The system found **strong evidence** that the content was created by a human author and has **high confidence**. If you believe the result is incorrect, you may submit an appeal for review.

## Rate limiting

the limits you chose and your reasoning for those specific values. Name at least one specific type of content your system would likely get wrong and explain why — tied to a property of your signals, not a generic "it needs more data."

Rate limiting is applied to `POST /submit` with Flask-Limiter, keyed by client IP. I chose three layered limits, sized for one creator submitting their own work rather than a script flooding the system:

| Window     | Limit | Rationale                                                             |
| ---------- | ----- | --------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| per minute | 10    | Allows quick draft → revise → resubmit iteration; blocks rapid bursts |
| per hour   | 60    | A long, active editing session — well above normal human pace         |
| per day    | 100   | Generous daily ceiling that still caps sustained abuse       

When a limit is exceeded the endpoint returns HTTP `429 Too Many Requests` and the detection pipeline does not run, so no Groq call or audit entry is made. `POST /appeal` is intentionally not rate-limited: appeals are low-frequency and already gated behind needing a valid `content_id`.

```text

429  ← request 11 blocked: Too Many Requests
429  ← request 12 blocked
```

The type of content this system would likely get wrong is **polished, formal human writing** — for example an academic essay, or text from a non-native English speaker whose style is more formal than typical. This is tied to a property of the stylometric signal: it measures surface statistics, not authorship. Its proxies — high lexical sophistication (long words), low sentence-length variance (uniform sentences), and clause-heavy punctuation — are exactly the statistics it reads as AI-like, so genuinely human formal prose earns a high stylometric score and is pushed toward *Uncertain* or *Likely AI*.

## Known limitations

at least one specific type of content your system would likely misclassify and why

One misclassification case is **lightly-edited AI text** — AI-generated content that a human has revised. Human editing restores varied sentence lengths and a more human-sounding voice, so the stylometric signal sees "human" variance and the LLM reads a human register. Both signals are pulled down, and the combined score can land in *Likely Human* even though the text was AI-generated. This is a direct consequence of how the signals work: the LLM judges overall writing patterns (which editing alters), and stylometry judges surface statistics (which editing normalizes) — neither inspects authorship history.

A second case, noted under Rate limiting, is **polished, formal human writing** (academic prose, or formal writing from a non-native English speaker), which the stylometric signal scores as AI-like because its proxies for sophistication and uniformity overlap with the markers of AI text.
